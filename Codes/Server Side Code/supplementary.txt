**Christ in the Storm on the Lake of Galilee**  

*Christ in the Storm on the Lake of Galilee* is an oil-on-canvas masterpiece painted by Rembrandt van Rijn in 1633. It vividly portrays a dramatic biblical scene where Jesus calms a raging storm, capturing intense human emotion and the power of faith through Rembrandt’s signature use of light and movement. The painting was part of the Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum’s collection in Boston until it was stolen in 1990 during one of the most notorious art heists in history. Despite extensive investigations, the artwork remains missing, and its whereabouts are unknown. Though lost, the painting continues to captivate art lovers and historians, standing as a testament to Rembrandt’s genius and the enduring mystery of its disappearance.  

**Naval Battle for the Islands of Loz, 7 February 1813**

This oil-on-canvas painting by Louis-Philippe Crépin depicts the naval engagement between the French frigate Aréthuse and the British frigate HMS Amelia near the Îles de Los off Guinea's coast on 7 February 1813. The battle, part of the Napoleonic Wars, lasted four hours and resulted in significant damage and casualties on both sides, ending indecisively. Crépin's work captures the intensity of the confrontation, reflecting the Romanticism style's emphasis on dramatic and emotive scenes. The painting measures 258 by 162 centimeters and is part of the collection at the Palace of Versailles in France. 

**Court of Akbar from Akbarnama**  

The *Court of Akbar* is an exquisite painting from the *Akbarnama*, a historical manuscript commissioned by Emperor Akbar and written by Abul Fazl in the late 16th century. The artwork was created by Mughal court artists, including Basawan and Nar Singh, under Akbar’s patronage. It depicts a vibrant and detailed scene of Akbar’s court, showcasing the emperor seated in grandeur, surrounded by nobles, scholars, and diplomats. The painting captures the rich cultural and political life of the Mughal court, emphasizing Akbar’s role as a unifier and patron of the arts. Currently, many folios of the *Akbarnama* are housed in the Victoria and Albert Museum in London, where they offer a glimpse into the golden age of Mughal rule and artistry.  

**Washington Crossing the Delaware**

*Washington Crossing the Delaware* is an iconic oil-on-canvas painting created in 1851 by German-American artist Emanuel Leutze. The artwork depicts General George Washington's daring nighttime crossing of the Delaware River on December 25–26, 1776, during the American Revolutionary War. This pivotal event led to a surprise attack on Hessian forces in Trenton, New Jersey, marking a turning point in the struggle for independence. Leutze's painting, measuring approximately 378.5 by 647.7 centimeters, showcases Washington heroically standing at the boat's prow, surrounded by diverse members of the Continental Army, symbolizing unity and determination. The composition, though not entirely historically accurate, emphasizes the monumental nature of the event and has become a symbol of American patriotism. The original 1850 version was damaged by fire and later destroyed during World War II. The second version, completed in 1851, is housed in the Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York City, where it continues to inspire and captivate viewers with its dramatic portrayal of a defining moment in American history. 

The Great Wave off Kanagawa

The Great Wave off Kanagawa is a renowned woodblock print created by Japanese artist Katsushika Hokusai in 1831, as part of his series Thirty-Six Views of Mount Fuji. The artwork depicts a towering wave with Mount Fuji in the background, symbolizing nature’s immense power. The wave's claw-like crest and dynamic movement have made it one of the most iconic images in Japanese art. The print reflects elements of ukiyo-e style, blending Japanese tradition with subtle Western influences like linear perspective. Multiple original impressions of the print exist, and they are held in collections worldwide, including the Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York and the British Museum in London. This timeless piece continues to inspire global art and culture.

Hamsa Damayanti

Hamsa Damayanti is an iconic oil painting created in 1899 by renowned Indian artist Raja Ravi Varma. The artwork illustrates a poignant episode from the Mahabharata, where Damayanti, a princess of exceptional beauty and virtue, converses with a celestial swan (hamsa). In this narrative, the swan acts as a messenger, conveying the affections of King Nala to Damayanti, leading to their profound love story. Ravi Varma's depiction captures Damayanti's grace and the serene ambiance of the moment, reflecting his mastery in blending Indian themes with European realism. Measuring 150 by 83 centimeters, the painting showcases intricate details and vibrant colors, characteristic of Ravi Varma's style. 
Currently, the original painting is part of a private collection, but reproductions and prints are widely available, allowing art enthusiasts to appreciate this masterpiece globally.

Number 1, 1950 (Lavender Mist)

Number 1, 1950 (Lavender Mist) is an iconic abstract expressionist painting by American artist Jackson Pollock, created in 1950. This large-scale work, measuring 221 x 300 cm, exemplifies Pollock's innovative "drip technique," where he poured and splattered paint onto a canvas laid flat on the floor. The composition features a dense network of black, white, pale pink, teal, and gray lines and splatters, creating a dynamic and chaotic visual experience. Pollock's approach marked a significant departure from traditional painting methods, emphasizing the physical act of painting itself. He stated, "I prefer to tack the unstretched canvas to the hard wall or the floor. I need the resistance of a hard surface. On the floor, I am more at ease. I feel nearer, more a part of the painting, since this way I can walk around it, work from the four sides, and literally be in the painting." The painting is housed in the National Gallery of Art in Washington, D.C., where it continues to be celebrated as a masterpiece of 20th-century art.

**Mona Lisa**  

The *Mona Lisa* is a world-renowned portrait painted by Leonardo da Vinci between 1503 and 1506 during the Italian Renaissance. This oil-on-wood panel painting depicts Lisa Gherardini, the wife of Florentine merchant Francesco del Giocondo, and is celebrated for her enigmatic expression and the masterful use of sfumato — a technique that creates soft transitions between tones and colors. The artwork’s subtle details and lifelike presence have made it one of the most studied and revered paintings in history. Currently, the *Mona Lisa* is housed in the Louvre Museum in Paris, where millions of visitors admire it each year. It remains the property of the French Republic and is considered an invaluable cultural treasure.  

**Napoleon Crossing the Alps**  

*Napoleon Crossing the Alps* is an iconic oil painting created by Jacques-Louis David in 1801. It depicts Napoleon Bonaparte heroically leading his army through the Great St. Bernard Pass during his 1800 campaign against the Austrians. The painting shows Napoleon on a rearing horse, symbolizing his strength and determination, with his name engraved alongside historical conquerors like Hannibal and Charlemagne. David produced five versions of the painting, which are now displayed in various locations, including Château de Malmaison in France and the Belvedere in Vienna. The artwork remains a powerful symbol of leadership and ambition, capturing Napoleon’s rise as a military and political figure.

*Semper Augustus**

*Semper Augustus* was the most famous and coveted tulip variety during the Dutch "Tulip Mania" of the 17th century. Renowned for its striking red and white "broken" petals, this tulip's unique appearance resulted from a mosaic virus transmitted by aphids, which caused the vivid flame-like streaks. During the peak of Tulip Mania, a single Semper Augustus bulb reportedly sold for more than the cost of a house, exemplifying the speculative frenzy of that period. citeturn0search0 However, the same virus that bestowed its beauty also weakened the plant, leading to its eventual extinction. Today, while the original Semper Augustus no longer exists, its legacy endures. **Starry Night**  

*Starry Night* is a masterpiece painted by Vincent van Gogh in 1889 during his stay at the Saint-Paul-de-Mausole asylum in Saint-Rémy-de-Provence, France. This oil-on-canvas painting depicts a swirling night sky filled with stars above a quiet village, with a large cypress tree in the foreground. Van Gogh's bold, dynamic brushstrokes and vivid colors capture the emotional intensity he felt at the time, expressing a deep connection to nature and the cosmos. The painting is often interpreted as a reflection of Van Gogh’s inner turmoil and longing for peace. Currently, *Starry Night* is housed at the Museum of Modern Art (MoMA) in New York City, where it remains one of the most celebrated and influential works of art in history.  

**The Astronomer**  

*The Astronomer* is a famous painting by Johannes Vermeer, created around 1668. This oil-on-canvas work depicts a scholarly figure studying a celestial globe, symbolizing humanity’s quest for knowledge and the mysteries of the cosmos. The painting reflects the scientific curiosity of the Dutch Golden Age, emphasizing the importance of astronomy and exploration during that time. Vermeer’s masterful use of light highlights the thoughtful expression of the astronomer, while intricate details — like the open book and carefully rendered objects — add to the painting’s realism. Currently, *The Astronomer* is housed in the Louvre Museum in Paris, where it remains an iconic representation of scientific discovery through art.  

The Entrance to the Grand Canal, Venice

The Entrance to the Grand Canal, Venice is a breathtaking painting by Canaletto, created in the early 18th century. Canaletto, renowned for his detailed and luminous cityscapes, captures the grandeur of Venice’s iconic waterways with precision and elegance. The painting vividly portrays the bustling activity of boats and gondolas against the architectural splendor of Venetian palaces and churches. Canaletto's mastery of light and perspective brings the scene to life, emphasizing the city’s unique atmosphere. This artwork is now part of the collection at The National Gallery in London, where it continues to enchant viewers with its timeless depiction of Venice’s charm and vibrancy.  

**The Last Supper**  

*The Last Supper* is a monumental fresco painted by Leonardo da Vinci between 1495 and 1498. It depicts the moment Jesus announces that one of his disciples will betray him, capturing the emotional intensity and individual reactions of each figure. The painting’s composition, with Jesus at the center forming a triangular shape, exemplifies Leonardo’s mastery of perspective and balance. The work is located in the Convent of Santa Maria delle Grazie in Milan, Italy, where it remains a revered masterpiece of the Italian Renaissance. Despite deterioration over time, its influence on art and culture is immeasurable, symbolizing themes of sacrifice, faith, and human emotion.  

The Tower of Babel

The Tower of Babel is a famous painting by Pieter Bruegel the Elder, created in 1563. This oil-on-panel masterpiece illustrates the biblical story from Genesis, where humanity’s ambition to build a tower reaching heaven leads to divine intervention and the scattering of languages. Bruegel meticulously portrays the colossal, spiraling structure, blending Roman architecture with chaotic construction scenes. The painting symbolizes human pride and the inevitable downfall of overreaching ambition. Currently, one version of the artwork is housed in the Kunsthistorisches Museum in Vienna, Austria. Bruegel’s detailed realism and storytelling ability make this piece a powerful exploration of human nature and divine justice.
